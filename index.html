How Will I Know?

A compassionate guide to assessing quality of life and making difficult decisions for your beloved companion

Scroll to begin

As pet owners, we accept the loving responsibility of making decisions about the health and well-being of our animals. These may be pleasant decisions, like what food to choose or what type of activities we enjoy together. However, pet ownership also means being faced with difficult decisions, such as whether or not to go forward with surgery or treatment of a disease.


Often the hardest decisions we face regarding our pets involve geriatric and end-of-life care. The needs and beliefs of each family are different, and what might be the right choice for one pet may not be right for another. It is our hope that this guide will help and support you, whether you are considering a particular treatment plan or end-of-life care.

Making Difficult Decisions

Questions Before Treatment Decisions

When considering surgery, chemotherapy, or other significant treatments, ask yourself:

  • What will happen in the near future that will be significant to my pet? Can my pet's pain or anxiety about upcoming procedures be well-controlled while hospitalized or healing?
  • Is the procedure likely to give my pet good quality of life for some period of time? How do I feel about the medical information I've been given about my pet's prognosis?
  • Do I have the financial resources to go forward with the procedure and handle ongoing treatment expenses? Can I sacrifice from other areas of my family's budget to provide this care?
  • Do I feel like I have all the information I need to make this decision comfortably? Are there questions I need to ask my veterinarian?
  • Do I have the emotional and physical stamina to provide the ongoing care my pet needs? Am I ready to undertake care such as lifting, assisting with walking, bathroom functions, physical therapy, medication, feeding tubes, or overnight monitoring?
  • What is my personal "bottom line" with regard to my family's lifestyle and how it will be impacted by this decision?

Questions Before End-of-Life Decisions

When considering euthanasia or hospice care:

  • What are my religious, spiritual and personal beliefs regarding end-of-life care? Do I have a trusted friend, spiritual advisor or veterinarian who can help me make this decision?
  • Have I asked the questions I need to ask in order to feel knowledgeable about euthanasia? Do I have fears or concerns I would like to discuss with my veterinarian?
  • Are there friends or family members who are close to my pet and would like to be included in this decision, or who would like to be present?
  • What is the value for my pet to continue to live this way? What is the value for me or my family members? Is the value higher for us than for our pet?
  • If I elect to provide hospice care, are my veterinarian and I able to adequately control my pet's discomfort or anxiety? Will I be honest with myself if my pet's symptoms are no longer well-controlled?

Understanding Anticipatory Grief

When a pet goes through a serious illness or a decline in health, it is very natural for a pet owner to experience "anticipatory grief"‚Äîgrieving before the pet's death. Just like grief after a death, anticipatory grief involves physical, emotional and mental reactions that can create sadness, denial and difficulty processing information.

The impending loss of a pet may bring back emotions related to other losses or deaths you may have experienced, which compounds the difficult emotions. Know that these feelings are completely normal.

Understanding Euthanasia

There are many misconceptions about euthanasia that can make this difficult time even harder. Understanding the truth can help bring peace to your decision.

"My pet will die peacefully at home surrounded by family."

Only a very small percentage of pets experience a "good" death without our help. Many distressing changes can happen during the dying process: anxiety, difficulty breathing, severe nausea, and seizures. Many pets who are allowed to die naturally will do so overnight or while the owner is away, leading to regret that the pet was alone. At-home euthanasia is offered by many veterinarians and provides a controlled, pain-free option for owners who prefer end-of-life care to happen at home.

"I would be killing my pet."

Remember that your pet's illness, disease or injury is doing the harm to your pet. Euthanasia provides the opportunity to allow your pet to die humanely and with dignity. It is their illness which causes suffering; you are relieving that suffering in a loving and respectful way.

"Euthanasia is not natural."

If you have religious or spiritual beliefs about euthanasia, please be honest with your veterinary team so they may try to honor and respect your beliefs. Much of the medical care we provide our pets is, by definition, interrupting a "natural" process or illness. As Moira Anderson Allen wrote: "Euthanasia is often not so much a question of 'artificially ending' a life, but of when to cease artificially extending that life."

"My pet will know what is happening."

When handled properly, the euthanasia process is a loving, peaceful, dignified end of a pet's life. Veterinarians can give medications including sedation and pain relief that will ease your pet's anxiety and pain, often inducing deep sleep just prior to the euthanasia injection. Animals live in the present moment. Unlike us, they don't know that a choice is being made‚Äîthey only know that they are being loved, that you are with them, and that they don't feel pain anymore.

"I will just know when it's time." or "My pet will let me know."

While there are sometimes signs, events or symptoms that make the choice obvious, often the choice is not clear. Many families tell us that they waited too long; families rarely tell us they made the decision too soon. Please know that it is normal and natural to second-guess a decision and that second-guessing does not mean you made the wrong choice. In cases of illness or declining quality of life, there is truly no wrong decision to be made, only the decision you feel is best for your pet and your family.

"It can feel odd or wrong to schedule a time for your pet's euthanasia. By scheduling ahead, you are taking some control over a situation over which you have little power. Many owners find comfort in scheduling a time when friends or family members can be present, or before their pet is truly suffering."

Enlist Help from Others You Trust

It may be helpful to ask those closest to you (and your pet) for their honest opinion about your pet's health and quality of life. Consider asking someone who sees your pet less frequently, since gradual decline may be more obvious to them.

Your veterinarian can provide valuable information about treatment options and prognosis. While they cannot make the euthanasia decision for you, they can give you an honest medical perspective about your pet's current condition and comfort.

Supporting Your Family

How Euthanasia Affects Children

Children can learn valuable lessons about responsibility, compassion, commitment, and coping when they are involved in the decision-making process regarding a family pet. Use your judgment as a parent regarding your child's personality, age, and level of development.

Important Language Guidance

Avoid the phrase "put to sleep," since young children may interpret this literally and become fearful of sleep for themselves or other loved ones. This phrase may also create confusion about anesthesia for routine procedures. Using the simple term "die" or "help to die peacefully" may eliminate confusion.

If you decide to have children present for the euthanasia, prepare them ahead of time for what to expect. Use language that is simple and clear. For example: "The veterinarian is going to give [pet's name] medicine that will make their heart stop beating and make them stop breathing. It will not hurt them."

Most importantly, it is okay to show your emotions. Allow your children to see that being sad is normal and natural, so they feel invited to express and share their own feelings. This has been shown to ease the grieving process for children and adults alike.

How Euthanasia Affects Other Pets

Some owners have reported fewer disruptions in their surviving animal's behavior when the surviving pet was allowed to see and smell the deceased pet after euthanasia.

If you are considering having your other pet present during euthanasia, an important consideration is that your emotions may startle your surviving pet. On the other hand, some owners feel comforted by their presence.

Keep in mind that you cannot predict how your surviving pet will react. Behaviors that may seem disrespectful‚Äîsuch as stepping on, urinating on, or barking at the deceased pet‚Äîare all normal behaviors for animals, especially if they sense your stress. Changes in the social structure of pets still in the home can be disrupted by a loss, and they may struggle to determine their new place.

"When you have decided on the time for the euthanasia, you may choose to be present or not. There is no right or wrong choice, only a loving choice."

Treasuring Your Time

If you have decided that euthanasia is the most appropriate choice, you may choose a variety of ways to spend the last months, weeks, or days of your companion animal's life:

  • Spending extra time together with additional petting, grooming, or holding
  • Making them special meals to eat (check with your veterinarian about what would be suitable)
  • Going on a special trip or walk together
  • Simply being present with them in quiet, comfortable moments
